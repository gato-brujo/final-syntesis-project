What is Red-shift?

In physics and astronomy, red-shift is a phenomenon that occurs when electromagnetic radiation that is emitted or reflected from an object, usually visible light, appears redshifted at the end of the electromagnetic spectrum. When we speak of red-shift, what we are indicating is that the waves that are perceived are a little closer to the type of waves of greater amplitude (radio waves); redshift is the displacement of the spectrum of an astronomical object toward longer (red) wavelengths. It is attributed to the Doppler effect, a change in wavelength that results when a given source of waves (ex: light or radio waves) and an observer are in motion with respect to each other. The Doppler effect causes stars to take on a near-red hue from Earth, the farther away a galaxy is, and in the infrared, the redder it will appear (from the point at which they pass from red to infrared, they are no longer visible to the human eye).

What is Blue-shift

A phenomenon that occurs when the frequency of a ray of light emitted by an object, approaching or moving toward to the observer, is received by the observer shifted toward to blue, it means its frequency increased. When we speak of blue-shift we are referring to the opposite effect of red-shift, we are closer to the waves of shorter length and it shifts toward the blue end of the spectrum. Is the decrease in wavelength of electromagnetic radiation received by an observer approaching the source emitting the radiation. This event is used in astronomy to determine the motion of celestial objects relative to the Earth. If the object is bluish, it is most likely approaching.
